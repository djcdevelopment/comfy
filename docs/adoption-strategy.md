# Adoption Strategy — the real barrier is political, not technical

> The hardest part of this project is not building or running the tool. It's that **legibility
> disrupts existing power structures.** This doc records the strategy and turns it into design
> constraints. (Origin: Derek's observation that adoption threatens leaders who built/control the
> current systems and derive status from gatekeeping their knowledge.)

## The barrier

Leaders who own a subsystem's knowledge and gatekeep it derive **status and control** from being
indispensable. A tool that makes that knowledge self-serve threatens them. **You cannot fully avoid
this.** The goal is to (a) reduce the threat by design and (b) change the political sequencing.

## Why grassroots value works: labor asymmetry

The people **threatened** (owner-leaders) and the people who **feel the daily pain** (reps doing
repetitive turn-in / rank-up / quest processing) are **different people.** The tool removes the
reps' toil, and a single rep can run it on their own workflow **without anyone's permission.** So
value is proven at the bottom *before* it reaches the political layer — turning top-down "approve
this disruption" (**push**, triggers defense) into bottom-up "we already rely on this" (**pull**,
hard to kill).

## Design levers that reduce the threat (partial answers to "how do you avoid it")

1. **Standardize the plumbing, never the content.** Each community defines its own wildly-different
   systems (the 7 guild ladders share almost nothing — that's sacred). The framework standardizes
   only the *format*, and renders back into *their* look/vocabulary/bot strings. Nothing organic
   gets flattened into a template → most of the threat evaporates. You give each leader a better way
   to be *themselves*, which they still own.
2. **Owner-controlled, opt-in — NOT surveillance.** The volunteer runs recipes on their *own*
   artifacts and owns the output. Never a top-down audit tool (that guarantees resistance). It's the
   steward's own mirror, not the org's microscope. (Sharp edge of "who watches the watchers": the
   answer must be "not this tool.")
3. **Adoptable in isolation.** Forkable per guild; one willing guild/rep needs no org-wide buy-in.
4. **Amplify the author.** Rendered outputs center the leader as source of truth. Offer the durable
   kind of power (recognized authorship + preserved legacy) in place of the fragile kind (being a
   stressed bottleneck).
5. **Labor-first sequencing.** Build recipes that remove REP toil first; leadership-facing
   dashboards later or never. Serve the advocates, not the gatekeepers.

## Who to target first

The **exhausted, not the entrenched.** Many leaders are burned-out volunteers drowning in
repetitive work and quietly afraid their knowledge dies when they step down (Bruce's story = heavy
labor, fragile trust). They are the first champions.

## Timing wedge

The **1.0 surge.** A newbie flood spikes rep toil and makes the bottleneck undeniable — grassroots
demand peaks, resistance weakens. Time adoption pushes to it.

## Realism

You won't convert everyone; you don't need to. Open-source + forkable + adoptable-in-isolation means
a few beachheads suffice. Clean results create envy (drifting rank *image* vs. crisp rank *page*).
**Adoption spreads by demonstration, not decree. Route around the immovable; let results recruit.**
